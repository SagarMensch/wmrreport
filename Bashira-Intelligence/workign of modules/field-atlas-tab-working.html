How the Field Atlas Tab Works

Field Atlas is the spatial operating-intelligence surface of the product. It takes the rig-risk enriched well roster, positions wells in coordinate space, estimates neighborhood pressure, clusters zones, computes high-pressure corridors between successive rig commitments, and turns the result into an interactive map and topology inspector. This tab is where well risk becomes spatial logistics intelligence.

1) Runtime Path

The frontend component is FieldAtlas.tsx. It loads through useCommandCenterData("field_atlas"), which calls the shared proxy route /api/command-center?action=field_atlas. The backend resolves this to /api/command-center/field-atlas and returns CommandCenterService.get_field_atlas_view().

Field Atlas therefore sits on top of the same command-center architecture as Portfolio, Smart Alerts, and Rig Operations. But instead of aggregating by project or rig first, it reprojects the operating frame into physical space.

2) Why This Tab Exists

Standard dashboards can tell you that wells are risky. They usually cannot show whether risky wells are geographically concentrated, whether neighboring wells create localized pressure fields, or whether the same rig is about to relay delay across the field.

  • Portfolio asks: which projects are under pressure?
  • Rig Operations asks: which rig queues are stressed?
  • Field Atlas asks: where is operational pressure physically concentrating, and how does it propagate spatially?

3) Upstream Dependency: Rig Operations Enrichment

Field Atlas does not invent its own risk layer from scratch. It first calls get_rig_operations_view() and builds a well_lookup map from the rig payload. That lookup injects into each positioned well:

  • ops_risk_score
  • ops_risk_tier
  • recovery_confidence_pct
  • queue_exposure
  • dominant_bottleneck
  • anomaly_flag
  • badges, evidence, actions, and score_breakdown

This means Field Atlas is not a detached geospatial page. It is the spatial extension of the operational risk stack built in Rig Operations.

4) Coordinate Preparation and Fallback Strategy

The service starts from the canonical operating frame and looks for real easting and northing values. Wells with valid coordinates are considered directly positioned.

For wells missing coordinates, the service computes cluster centroids from the wells in the same cluster that do have coordinates. If such a centroid exists, the missing well is assigned the centroid position and marked with:

  • coord_source = "actual" for real coordinates
  • coord_source = "cluster_imputed" for centroid-filled coordinates

Wells that still cannot be positioned are dropped from the atlas. This gives the tab graceful degradation on incomplete local data.

5) Atlas Record Construction

Once coordinates are available, the service builds an atlas row for each well containing:

  • identity: well, project, cluster, rig, well type
  • baseline risk and progress
  • schedule delay and rig-on delay
  • stage completion percentages
  • expected and actual rig dates
  • coordinate source and positioned coordinates

The rig-derived fields are then joined on top of this atlas row, so each positioned well carries both physical location and operational state.

6) Fallback Logic for Missing Rig-Enriched Fields

If a well is missing rig-enriched attributes, the atlas applies fallback rules:

Field Fallback
ops_risk_score Falls back to the original risk_score
ops_risk_tier Falls back to the original risk_tier
recovery_confidence_pct Falls back to 100 - ops_risk_score, clipped into a practical range

This design lets the tab remain usable even if some upstream enrichment is absent.

7) Adaptive Neighborhood Radius

Atlas uses nearest-neighbor modeling in two phases. First it calibrates a radius from the wells with actual coordinates. It uses NearestNeighbors with up to six neighbors, examines the neighbor-distance distribution, and sets:

neighbor_radius = clamp(quantile_0.75(neighbor_distance) * 1.25, 1200, 4200)

This matters because the field may have different density characteristics depending on the local dataset. A fixed radius would be brittle.

8) Neighborhood Pressure Model

With a calibrated radius, Atlas runs a radius-neighbor search over all positioned wells. For every well it computes:

  • local_neighbor_count: number of nearby wells
  • nearby_critical_count: nearby wells in critical or high-risk tier
  • neighborhood_pressure_score: weighted average of nearby wells’ operational risk scores

Neighbor weights decay with distance:

weight = max(0.08, 1 - distance / neighbor_radius)

This is a local field-pressure model. Close risky wells matter more than distant risky wells.

9) Spatial Signal Score Formula

The core metric of Field Atlas is spatial_signal_score. This is the quantity that determines which wells rise to the top of the atlas and which neighborhoods become exposed.

spatial_signal_score = 0.52 * ops_risk_score + 0.23 * neighborhood_pressure_score + 0.10 * local_density_score + 2.2 * queue_exposure + 2.4 * nearby_critical_count + 6 * anomaly_flag

The final value is clipped to the range 0-99.

Term Meaning
ops_risk_score Intrinsic operational pressure of the well from Rig Operations
neighborhood_pressure_score Average pressure of nearby wells, weighted by distance
local_density_score Relative concentration of neighboring wells
queue_exposure How many downstream wells share this rig queue
nearby_critical_count How many nearby wells are already highly stressed
anomaly_flag Boolean lift for execution outlier status

This is why the tab is useful: a well can become spatially important not only because it is risky itself, but because it sits in a risky neighborhood or in a risky transition corridor.

10) Adaptive Spatial Zoning with DBSCAN

Field Atlas then groups actual-position wells into zones using DBSCAN when enough positioned wells are available. The eps radius is adapted from the neighborhood radius:

eps = clamp(neighbor_radius * 1.28, 1500, 5200)

Only actual-coordinate wells are used to seed these zones. The algorithm discovers organically dense spatial groups without forcing a preset number of zones.

11) Zone Assignment and Fallback

Once DBSCAN finds actual-coordinate groups, the service computes a centroid and radius envelope for each zone. Every atlas well is then assigned to its nearest zone if it falls within a threshold. Imputed-coordinate wells are given a looser threshold because their position is less certain.

If no DBSCAN zone can be confidently assigned, the well falls back to a cluster-derived zone ID:

  • Zxx for DBSCAN-derived zones
  • C-cluster for cluster fallback zones

12) Zone Objects and What They Mean

Zone objects are then constructed with:

  • zone center and spatial radii
  • well count and active well count
  • rig count and listed rigs
  • critical wells, delayed wells, anomaly wells
  • average signal score, average risk score, average progress
  • average recovery confidence
  • total queue exposure
  • dominant bottleneck
  • top wells by spatial signal

Zones are sorted by average signal score, then critical-well count, then delayed-well count. That makes them candidate operational theaters.

13) Hotspot Cells

In addition to adaptive zones, the service creates a simpler hotspot lattice by dividing the coordinate extent into a coarse fixed grid. Each well is assigned to:

  • grid_x = ((easting - min_x) / x_span) * 5
  • grid_y = ((northing - min_y) / y_span) * 3

These grid cells are aggregated into hotspot objects carrying average signal score, average risk, delayed wells, critical wells, and queue exposure. Hotspots are useful as a stable executive heat layer even when adaptive zones vary with data density.

14) Corridor Construction

The corridor model is one of the most valuable parts of the atlas. For each rig, the service orders wells by expected rig-on date, then by delay and risk, and links successive wells. Each link becomes a possible operational corridor.

A corridor therefore represents the transition from one rig commitment to the next under the same rig.

15) Corridor Pressure Formula

Each corridor receives a pressure_score:

pressure_score = min(99, 0.62 * max(spatial_signal_current, spatial_signal_next) + 1.45 * combined_delay + 4.6 * max(0, -handover_gap_days) + min(18, distance_m / 850) + 1.8 * max(queue_exposure_current, queue_exposure_next))

That means corridor pressure rises when:

  • either end of the corridor is already spatially stressed
  • the two wells carry large delay values
  • the next well is scheduled before the prior one can realistically hand over
  • the physical distance is large enough to complicate transfer
  • queue exposure is already high

Corridors are classified as:

  • Sequence Clash if handover gap is negative
  • Pressure Relay if corridor pressure is high
  • Transition Corridor otherwise

16) Returned Payload

The backend returns:

  • summary: coverage, zones, corridors, exposed wells, and average spatial signal
  • bounds: coordinate extent for rendering
  • wells: positioned wells sorted by spatial signal
  • clusters: cluster-level centroids and aggregate metrics
  • zones: adaptive spatial operating theaters
  • hotspots: coarse-grid exposure cells
  • corridors: rig transition links ranked by pressure score

This payload is rich enough that the frontend can stay client-driven after one fetch.

17) Summary Metrics

The summary includes:

  • spatial_coverage_pct
  • critical_positioned
  • hotspot_cells
  • rig_corridors
  • zone_count
  • avg_spatial_signal_score
  • exposed_wells

18) Frontend Rendering Architecture

Unlike some other tabs, Field Atlas does not use Plotly for the main spatial canvas. The key renderer is a custom SVG engine called AtlasCanvas.

It supports two modes:

  • 2d-map: flat coordinate extent view
  • 3d-topology: isometric lifted topology where vertical lift corresponds to operational risk

This is important for product identity. The map is not a generic GIS embed. It is a designed quantitative canvas with custom geometry.

19) What the 3D Mode Really Is

The 3D mode is not a geospatial WebGL simulation. It is a custom isometric projection rendered in SVG. Each well is projected into a pseudo-3D coordinate system, lifted by its risk, and connected to the floor plane. Corridors are rendered as projected lines on the same surface.

So when the UI says 3D Topology, it means a volumetric analytical representation of spatial pressure, not a literal terrain engine. This is still useful because it makes relative spatial concentration legible without introducing a heavy 3D dependency.

20) Interaction Model

The frontend keeps interaction local after the payload loads:

  • search by well or project
  • tier filtering
  • mode toggle between 2D and 3D
  • corridor visibility toggle
  • entity selection for well or corridor
  • fullscreen chart overlay

The selected entity drives the right-hand dossier, which can display evidence, queue exposure, recovery confidence, and corridor detail.

21) Business Value of the Tab

Field Atlas creates value because operations are spatial. If execution pressure clusters geographically or propagates through specific rig corridors, that changes how crews, logistics, sequencing, and managerial attention should be allocated.

  • It converts abstract risk into field layout
  • It reveals local concentration instead of only global averages
  • It surfaces relay risk between successive rig commitments
  • It lets leadership reason about physical operating theaters rather than isolated records

22) The Most Important Architectural Insight

Field Atlas is not a separate geospatial subsystem with unrelated logic. It is the spatial projection of the same operational risk stack already computed in Rig Operations. The tab takes that well-level ops risk, embeds it in geographic context, blends it with neighbor density and local criticality, then elevates the result into zones, hotspots, and rig corridors.

In plain terms: Rig Operations explains which wells and rigs are stressed. Field Atlas explains where that stress lives in the field and how it may spread.

Field Atlas is a hybrid spatial pressure model: rig-risk enrichment, adaptive neighborhood analysis, DBSCAN-based zoning, hotspot lattice aggregation, and corridor-based sequence pressure, all rendered through a custom 2D/3D topology canvas.
